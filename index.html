Why we show our faces

Every garment we make carries a name, not just a label. These are the three hands that shaped the one you're holding.

Weaver · Tanti

Nasima Begum

Keeper of the loom, 19 years at the trade

Nasima learned the rhythm of the shuttle from her mother before she was ten, and today she is one of the few hands left in her district who can still lay a true Jamdani motif by memory alone. She reads a length of khadi cotton the way others read a page — by feel, by weight, by the give of the weave. Every bolt she finishes carries the same unhurried precision her grandmother once taught her.

Block Printer · Tailor

Rupali Akter

Precision cutter, block print specialist

Rupali works in a steady rhythm — measure, mark, cut, press — and it shows in seams that sit flat and hems that never wander. She trained first on hand-block printing, tapping carved teak blocks into repeat across metres of cloth without a single motif drifting out of line. That same eye for exactness now guides every garment she finishes.

Embroiderer · Karigar

Shirin Sultana

Hand-needlework artisan, Nakshi Kantha technique

Shirin's stitches are slow by design — the same running-stitch patience of the Nakshi Kantha quilts her family has made for three generations, turned here into fine details on cloth. She works entirely by hand, no machine, tracing motifs of leaves and lattice that take her hours to complete and a lifetime to master. She says a garment isn't finished until the stitches disappear into the story.
